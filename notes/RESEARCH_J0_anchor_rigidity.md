# J0-Anchored Rigidity for Two-Term and Low-Dimensional Oscillatory Profiles

## Goal

The universal-shift-series note shows that if two active frequencies occur in a common
multi-frequency infinite-resonance configuration, then their higher zero-shift series must be
generated by one universal normalized formal series.

The simplest nontrivial test case is to anchor one active frequency at the pure vertical profile
`J_0` and ask whether a different one-frequency profile can share the same normalized shift
geometry.

For the first low-dimensional oscillatory families, the answer is no: both
\[
b_0J_0+b_1J_1
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
b_0J_0+b_1J_1+b_2J_2
\]
collapse back to scalar multiples of `J_0` under the universal-shift constraint.

Moreover, the two-term analysis now extends much further: once one active frequency is anchored
at `J_0`, no nontrivial mixed two-term family
\[
b_0J_0+b_kJ_k
\qquad (k\ge 1,\ b_0\neq 0)
\]
can share the same universal normalized shift geometry.

## Odd two-term zero-shift coefficients

We first treat the general mixed two-term family
\[
F_{b_0,b_\ell}(t)=b_0J_0(t)+b_\ell J_\ell(t),
\qquad
\ell=2r+1\ge 1.
\]

Assume `b_0\neq 0`, and write
\[
a:=4\ell^2-1,
\qquad
y:=(-1)^r\frac{b_\ell}{b_0}.
\]

Using the standard Hankel expansions,
\[
J_0(t)=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi t}}
\left(
\cos\xi+\frac{1}{8t}\sin\xi-\frac{9}{128t^2}\cos\xi+O(t^{-3})
\right),
\]
\[
J_\ell(t)=(-1)^r\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi t}}
\left(
\sin\xi+\frac{a}{8t}\cos\xi-\frac{a(a-8)}{128t^2}\sin\xi+O(t^{-3})
\right),
\]
with `\xi=t-\pi/4`, one obtains
\[
F_{b_0,b_\ell}(t)
=
b_0\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi t}}
\left(
\left(1+\frac{ay}{8t}-\frac{9}{128t^2}+O(t^{-3})\right)\cos\xi
+
\left(y+\frac{1}{8t}-\frac{a(a-8)y}{128t^2}+O(t^{-3})\right)\sin\xi
\right).
\]

After rotating by the leading phase `\phi`, where
\[
\rho:=\sqrt{1+y^2},
\qquad
\cos\phi=\frac{1}{\rho},
\qquad
\sin\phi=\frac{y}{\rho},
\]
the formal zero shift
\[
\psi=\frac{\pi}{2}+\lambda_1 t^{-1}+\lambda_2 t^{-2}+O(t^{-3}),
\qquad
\psi=\xi-\phi,
\]
satisfies
\[
\lambda_1=\frac{1-ay^2}{8(1+y^2)},
\]
and
\[
\lambda_2=
\frac{y(a+1)\bigl((a+9)y^2-(a-7)\bigr)}
{128(1+y^2)^2}.
\]

## The anchor profile `J_0`

For the pure vertical profile `J_0`, the same computation gives
\[
\lambda_1(J_0)=\frac18,
\qquad
\lambda_2(J_0)=0.
\]

## Anchored rigidity for odd two-term families

### Theorem 1

Let `n,p` be two distinct positive active frequencies in a hypothetical common infinite-resonance
configuration. Suppose the `n`th active frequency carries the profile `J_0`, while the `p`th
active frequency carries a mixed odd two-term profile
\[
F_{b_0,b_\ell}(t)=b_0J_0(t)+b_\ell J_\ell(t),
\qquad
\ell=2r+1\ge 1,
\qquad
b_0\neq 0.
\]

If the two active frequencies share the same universal normalized shift series, then
\[
b_\ell=0.
\]
Equivalently, the second profile must also be a scalar multiple of `J_0`.

### Proof

By the universal-shift-series law, the normalized higher zero-shift coefficients must agree:
\[
\frac{\lambda_r(F_{b_0,b_\ell})}{p^{r+1}}
=
\frac{\lambda_r(J_0)}{n^{r+1}}
\qquad (r\ge 1).
\]
For `r=2`, this gives
\[
\lambda_2(F_{b_0,b_\ell})=0.
\]
Using the explicit formula above, either `y=0`, in which case `b_\ell=0`, or
\[
y^2=\frac{a-7}{a+9}.
\]
If `\ell=1`, then `a=3`, and the second alternative would give
\[
y^2=-\frac13,
\]
which is impossible over `\R`. So in this case already `y=0`.

Assume now `\ell\ge 3`, so `a\ge 35`. Then the nonzero branch gives a real value of `y`, and we
can use the order-`1` normalized equality. Set
\[
q:=\frac{p^2}{n^2}>0.
\]
Since `8\lambda_1=q`, we obtain
\[
q=\frac{1-ay^2}{1+y^2}.
\]
Substituting
\[
y^2=\frac{a-7}{a+9}
\]
gives
\[
q
=
\frac{1-a\frac{a-7}{a+9}}{1+\frac{a-7}{a+9}}
=
-\frac{(a-9)(a-1)}{2(a+1)}.
\]
For `a\ge 35`, the right-hand side is strictly negative, contradicting `q>0`.

Therefore only `y=0` is possible, hence `b_\ell=0`.

### Meaning

This is a substantial strengthening of the first anchored rigidity statement.

It says that once one active frequency is anchored at the pure vertical profile `J_0`, a second
active frequency cannot come from any nontrivial mixed odd two-term family
\[
b_0J_0+b_\ell J_\ell
\qquad (\ell\ \text{odd},\ b_0\neq 0)
\]
and still share the same universal normalized shift geometry.

So the bad locus is already empty on an infinite odd two-term series, not just on the lowest
family `b_0J_0+b_1J_1`.

### Corollary 1

In particular, for the lowest odd family
\[
F_{b_0,b_1}(t)=b_0J_0(t)+b_1J_1(t),
\]
anchored universal-shift compatibility with `J_0` forces
\[
b_1=0.
\]

## Research meaning

This theorem is no longer just a lowest-order sanity check.

The generic finite-resonance theorem says that the non-shared bad locus is thin. The anchored
rigidity theorem now shows that on an entire infinite odd two-term series that thin locus is in
fact empty.

That is evidence that the ultimate sharp theorem may well be true:

> two genuinely non-synchronized effective active frequencies cannot share the same universal
> normalized shift series.

## Next step

The obvious next targets are:

1. replace the anchor `J_0` by more general one-frequency profiles with explicit shift data;
2. move from low-dimensional anchored families to a structural criterion forcing the universal
   shift series to determine the profile projectively;
3. try to formulate an abstract criterion forcing the universal shift series to determine the
   profile projectively, at least in low-dimensional quotient sectors.

Any of these would push the current generic theorem toward a sharp nonexistence theorem.

## Three-term anchored rigidity

We now push the anchored argument one step further and replace the second profile by
\[
F_{b_0,b_1,b_2}(t)=b_0J_0(t)+b_1J_1(t)+b_2J_2(t),
\qquad
(b_0,b_1,b_2)\neq (0,0,0).
\]

The goal is the same: determine whether such a three-term profile can share the same universal
normalized shift series as the anchor profile `J_0`.

### Three-term zero-shift coefficients

Write
\[
A=b_0-b_2,
\qquad
B=b_1,
\qquad
\rho:=\sqrt{A^2+B^2}.
\]
Using the Hankel expansions of `J_0,J_1,J_2`, one obtains after rotation by the leading phase
\[
F_{b_0,b_1,b_2}(t)
=
\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi t}}
\left(
(\rho+U_1t^{-1}+U_2t^{-2}+U_3t^{-3})\cos\psi
+
(V_1t^{-1}+V_2t^{-2}+V_3t^{-3})\sin\psi
+
O(t^{-4})
\right),
\]
where `\psi=\xi-\phi` and
\[
C=\frac{3b_1}{8},
\qquad
D=\frac{b_0+15b_2}{8},
\]
\[
E=\frac{-9b_0+105b_2}{128},
\qquad
F=\frac{15b_1}{128},
\]
\[
G=\frac{-315b_1}{3072},
\qquad
J=\frac{-225b_0+945b_2}{3072}.
\]
The first coefficients needed for the zero-shift expansion are
\[
U_1=\frac{AC+BD}{\rho},
\qquad
V_1=\frac{AD-BC}{\rho},
\]
\[
V_2=\frac{-EB+FA}{\rho}.
\]
Therefore
\[
\lambda_1=\frac{V_1}{\rho}
=
\frac{b_0^2+14b_0b_2-15b_2^2-3b_1^2}
{8\bigl((b_0-b_2)^2+b_1^2\bigr)},
\]
and
\[
\lambda_2=\frac{V_2-U_1\lambda_1}{\rho}
=
\frac{b_1\bigl(3(b_0-5b_2)-8(b_0+3b_2)\lambda_1\bigr)}
{16\bigl((b_0-b_2)^2+b_1^2\bigr)}.
\]

For the anchor profile `J_0`, we also use the third coefficient
\[
\lambda_3(J_0)=-\frac{25}{384}.
\]

### Theorem 2

Let `n,m` be two distinct positive active frequencies in a hypothetical common infinite-resonance
configuration. Suppose the `n`th active frequency carries the profile `J_0`, while the `m`th
active frequency carries the three-term profile
\[
F_{b_0,b_1,b_2}(t)=b_0J_0(t)+b_1J_1(t)+b_2J_2(t),
\qquad
(b_0,b_1,b_2)\neq (0,0,0).
\]

If the two active frequencies share the same universal normalized shift series, then
\[
b_1=b_2=0.
\]
Equivalently, the second profile must again be a scalar multiple of `J_0`.

### Proof

Set
\[
q:=\frac{m^2}{n^2}>0.
\]
The universal-shift-series law gives, for every `r\ge 1`,
\[
\lambda_r(F_{b_0,b_1,b_2})
=
\frac{m^{r+1}}{n^{r+1}}\lambda_r(J_0).
\]
In particular,
\[
\lambda_1=\frac{q}{8},
\qquad
\lambda_2=0,
\qquad
\lambda_3=-\frac{25}{384}q^2.
\]

We distinguish cases.

#### Case 1: `b_2=0`

Then
\[
F_{b_0,b_1,b_2}=b_0J_0+b_1J_1,
\]
and Theorem 1 applies directly. Hence `b_1=0`.

#### Case 2: `b_2\neq 0`

Scale by `b_2` and write
\[
x:=\frac{b_0}{b_2},
\qquad
y:=\frac{b_1^2}{b_2^2}\ge 0.
\]

##### Subcase 2a: `b_1=0`

Then the profile is
\[
b_2(xJ_0+J_2).
\]
In this same-parity branch one computes
\[
\lambda_1=\frac{1+15x}{8(1-x)},
\qquad
\lambda_2=0,
\qquad
\lambda_3=-\frac{x^3+9x^2-45x+135}{1536}.
\]
Since `\lambda_1=q/8`, we obtain
\[
q=\frac{1+15x}{1-x}.
\]
Eliminating `x` yields
\[
\lambda_3=-\frac{q^3+9q^2-45q+135}{1536}.
\]
Comparing with the anchored identity
\[
\lambda_3=-\frac{25}{384}q^2=-\frac{100}{1536}q^2
\]
gives
\[
q^3-91q^2-45q+135=0.
\]
This factors as
\[
(q-1)(q^2-90q-135)=0.
\]
Since `q=(m/n)^2` is a positive rational square, the only admissible root is `q=1`. Then
\[
\frac{1+15x}{1-x}=1
\]
forces `x=0`. Hence this subcase yields only scalar multiples of `J_0`.

##### Subcase 2b: `b_1\neq 0`

From `\lambda_2=0` and the explicit formula above,
\[
3(b_0-5b_2)-8(b_0+3b_2)\lambda_1=0.
\]
Using `\lambda_1=q/8`, this becomes
\[
q=\frac{3(x-5)}{x+3}.
\]
On the other hand, the formula for `\lambda_1` gives
\[
q
=
\frac{x^2+14x-15-3y}{(x-1)^2+y}.
\]
Eliminating `q` yields
\[
y=\frac{-x^2+18x+15}{3}.
\]
Hence `y\ge 0` implies
\[
9-4\sqrt6\le x\le 9+4\sqrt6.
\]
Since `q>0`, the identity
\[
q=\frac{3(x-5)}{x+3}
\]
forces `x>5`.

Along this locus the third coefficient simplifies to
\[
\lambda_3=
\frac{-5x^3+3x^2+345x+105}{128(x+3)^3}.
\]
Comparing with
\[
\lambda_3=-\frac{25}{384}q^2
=
-\frac{75(x-5)^2}{128(x+3)^2}
\]
gives
\[
35x^3-261x^2-15x+2865=0.
\]
Define
\[
P(x):=35x^3-261x^2-15x+2865.
\]
Then
\[
P'(x)=105x^2-522x-15.
\]
The larger critical point is
\[
\frac{87+4\sqrt{477}}{35}<5.
\]
Therefore `P` is strictly increasing on `[5,\infty)`. Since
\[
P(5)=640>0,
\]
we obtain `P(x)>0` for all `x>5`, contradiction.

So this subcase is impossible.

All cases lead to the conclusion
\[
b_1=b_2=0.
\]

### Meaning

This is the first anchored emptiness theorem for a genuinely mixed three-term family.

It shows that once one effective active frequency is anchored at `J_0`, even the first nontrivial
three-term profile
\[
b_0J_0+b_1J_1+b_2J_2
\]
cannot share the same universal normalized shift series unless it collapses back to a scalar
multiple of `J_0`.

So the non-shared bad locus is already empty on a larger low-dimensional sector than was visible
from Theorem 1 alone.

## Updated research meaning

Taken together, Theorems 1 and 2 show that a `J_0` anchor is substantially more rigid than the
generic thinness theorem alone would suggest.

At least in the first genuinely non-shared low-dimensional sectors, the residual bad locus is
not merely meagre or measure zero: it is actually empty.

This strengthens the evidence for the sharper frontier statement:

> once one effective active frequency is anchored at `J_0`, a second genuinely non-synchronized
> effective active frequency should not be able to share the same universal normalized shift
> series at all.

## A same-parity nonconsecutive anchored theorem

The previous theorem handles the first mixed three-term family. It is also useful to record a
clean nonconsecutive same-parity result, because it shows that the anchored emptiness phenomenon
is not confined to the adjacent block `J_0,J_1,J_2`.

Consider
\[
F_{b_0,b_4}(t)=b_0J_0(t)+b_4J_4(t),
\qquad
(b_0,b_4)\neq (0,0).
\]

Using the Hankel expansions
\[
J_0(t)=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi t}}
\left(
\cos\xi+\frac{1}{8t}\sin\xi-\frac{9}{128t^2}\cos\xi-\frac{225}{3072t^3}\sin\xi+O(t^{-4})
\right),
\]
\[
J_4(t)=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi t}}
\left(
\cos\xi-\frac{63}{8t}\sin\xi-\frac{3465}{128t^2}\cos\xi+\frac{135135}{3072t^3}\sin\xi
+O(t^{-4})
\right),
\]
with `\xi=t-\pi/4`, one gets the following explicit shift coefficients.

### Proposition 3

Assume `b_0\neq 0`, and write
\[
x:=\frac{b_4}{b_0}.
\]
Then
\[
\lambda_1(F_{b_0,b_4})=\frac{1-63x}{8(1+x)},
\]
and
\[
\lambda_3(F_{b_0,b_4})
=
\frac{-25+17973x-49995x^2-2457x^3}{384(1+x)^3}.
\]

### Proof

The rotated cosine-sine expansion has the form
\[
F_{b_0,b_4}(t)
=
\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi t}}
\left(
(A_0+A_2t^{-2})\cos\xi+(B_1t^{-1}+B_3t^{-3})\sin\xi+O(t^{-4})
\right),
\]
with
\[
A_0=b_0+b_4,
\qquad
A_2=-\frac{9b_0+3465b_4}{128},
\]
\[
B_1=\frac{b_0-63b_4}{8},
\qquad
B_3=\frac{-225b_0+135135b_4}{3072}.
\]
If the zero shift is written as
\[
\delta=\lambda_1 t^{-1}+\lambda_3 t^{-3}+O(t^{-5}),
\]
then the formal zero equation
\[
-(A_0+A_2t^{-2})\sin\delta+(B_1t^{-1}+B_3t^{-3})\cos\delta=0
\]
gives
\[
\lambda_1=\frac{B_1}{A_0},
\qquad
\lambda_3=-\frac{A_2}{A_0}\lambda_1-\frac{\lambda_1^3}{3}+\frac{B_3}{A_0}.
\]
Substituting the coefficients above yields the formulas claimed.

### Theorem 3

Let `n,m` be two distinct positive active frequencies in a hypothetical common infinite-resonance
configuration. Suppose the `n`th active frequency carries the anchor profile `J_0`, while the
`m`th active frequency carries
\[
F_{b_0,b_4}(t)=b_0J_0(t)+b_4J_4(t),
\qquad
(b_0,b_4)\neq (0,0).
\]

If the two active frequencies share the same universal normalized shift series, then
\[
b_4=0.
\]
So the second profile must again be a scalar multiple of `J_0`.

### Proof

If `b_0=0`, then the second profile is a scalar multiple of `J_4`, and
\[
\lambda_1(J_4)=-\frac{63}{8}<0,
\]
which is incompatible with the positive anchor value `\lambda_1(J_0)=1/8` under normalized
equality. So we may assume `b_0\neq 0` and write `x=b_4/b_0`.

Set
\[
q:=\frac{m^2}{n^2}>0.
\]
The universal normalized shift law implies
\[
\lambda_1(F_{b_0,b_4})=\frac{q}{8},
\qquad
\lambda_3(F_{b_0,b_4})=-\frac{25}{384}q^2.
\]
From the formula for `\lambda_1`,
\[
q=\frac{1-63x}{1+x}.
\]
Substituting this into the `\lambda_3` identity and clearing denominators gives
\[
4096\,x\bigl(29+90x+189x^2\bigr)=0.
\]
The quadratic factor has discriminant
\[
90^2-4\cdot 189\cdot 29=-13824<0,
\]
so it is strictly positive for all real `x`. Hence `x=0`, i.e. `b_4=0`.

### Meaning

This gives a second genuinely non-shared anchored emptiness theorem beyond the adjacent families
treated above.

So the `J_0` anchor already kills:

1. the mixed two-term family `b_0J_0+b_1J_1`;
2. the mixed three-term family `b_0J_0+b_1J_1+b_2J_2`;
3. the same-parity nonconsecutive family `b_0J_0+b_4J_4`.

This is further evidence that the residual non-shared bad locus may in fact be empty, not merely
thin.

## General same-parity two-term reduction

The preceding `J_4` computation is not isolated. The same elimination works for every
same-parity two-term family
\[
F_{b_0,b_{2r}}(t)=b_0J_0(t)+b_{2r}J_{2r}(t),
\qquad r\ge 1.
\]

Write
\[
a:=16r^2-1,
\qquad
y:=(-1)^r\frac{b_{2r}}{b_0},
\]
assuming `b_0\neq 0`.

### Proposition 4

For the family `F_{b_0,b_{2r}}`, one has
\[
\lambda_1(F_{b_0,b_{2r}})=\frac{1-ay}{8(1+y)}.
\]
Moreover, if `F_{b_0,b_{2r}}` shares the same universal normalized shift series as the anchor
profile `J_0`, then `y` must satisfy
\[
y\Bigl(A_r+B_r y+C_r y^2\Bigr)=0,
\]
where
\[
A_r=a^3-29a^2-253a-223,
\]
\[
B_r=-a^3+157a^2-67a-225,
\]
\[
C_r=192a(a+1).
\]

### Proof

For `J_{2r}`, the standard Hankel expansion has the form
\[
J_{2r}(t)=(-1)^r\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi t}}
\left(
\cos\xi-\frac{a}{8t}\sin\xi-\frac{a(a-8)}{128t^2}\cos\xi
-\frac{a(a-8)(a-24)}{3072t^3}\sin\xi+O(t^{-4})
\right),
\]
with `\xi=t-\pi/4`.
Combining this with the `J_0` expansion gives
\[
F_{b_0,b_{2r}}(t)
=
\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi t}}
\left(
(A_0+A_2t^{-2})\cos\xi+(B_1t^{-1}+B_3t^{-3})\sin\xi+O(t^{-4})
\right),
\]
where
\[
A_0=b_0(1+y),
\qquad
B_1=\frac{b_0}{8}(1-ay),
\]
\[
A_2=-\frac{b_0}{128}\bigl(9+a(a-8)y\bigr),
\qquad
B_3=\frac{b_0}{3072}\bigl(-225+a(a-8)(a-24)y\bigr).
\]
Hence
\[
\lambda_1=\frac{B_1}{A_0}=\frac{1-ay}{8(1+y)}.
\]

Now suppose the anchor constraint holds. Setting
\[
q:=\frac{m^2}{n^2}>0,
\]
the universal normalized shift law gives
\[
\lambda_1(F_{b_0,b_{2r}})=\frac{q}{8},
\qquad
\lambda_3(F_{b_0,b_{2r}})=-\frac{25}{384}q^2.
\]
Eliminating `q` via `q=(1-ay)/(1+y)` and using
\[
\lambda_3=-\frac{A_2}{A_0}\lambda_1-\frac{\lambda_1^3}{3}+\frac{B_3}{A_0},
\]
one obtains after simplification exactly
\[
y\Bigl(A_r+B_r y+C_r y^2\Bigr)=0.
\]

### Corollary 2

For the family
\[
F_{b_0,b_6}(t)=b_0J_0(t)+b_6J_6(t),
\qquad
(b_0,b_6)\neq (0,0),
\]
anchored universal-shift compatibility with `J_0` forces
\[
b_6=0.
\]

### Proof

Here `r=3`, so `a=143` and `y=-b_6/b_0`. Proposition 4 gives
\[
y\bigl(2294784+276480y+3953664y^2\bigr)=0.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
\frac{b_6}{b_0}\Bigl(2294784-276480\frac{b_6}{b_0}+3953664\frac{b_6^2}{b_0^2}\Bigr)=0.
\]
The quadratic factor has discriminant
\[
276480^2-4\cdot 2294784\cdot 3953664<0,
\]
so it is strictly positive for all real `b_6/b_0`. Hence `b_6=0`.

This supplies another non-shared anchored emptiness theorem, now in the next same-parity
two-term direction beyond `J_4`.

### Theorem 4

Let `r\ge 1`. Suppose the `n`th active frequency carries the anchor profile `J_0`, while the
`m`th active frequency carries the same-parity two-term profile
\[
F_{b_0,b_{2r}}(t)=b_0J_0(t)+b_{2r}J_{2r}(t),
\qquad
(b_0,b_{2r})\neq (0,0).
\]

If the two active frequencies share the same universal normalized shift series, then
\[
b_{2r}=0.
\]
So the second profile must again be a scalar multiple of `J_0`.

### Proof

The case `r=1` is already contained in Theorem 2 via the same-parity branch `b_1=0`. The case
`r=2` is Theorem 3. So it remains to treat `r\ge 3`.

Assume toward contradiction that `b_{2r}\neq 0`. If `b_0=0`, then the second profile is a
scalar multiple of `J_{2r}`, and
\[
\lambda_1(J_{2r})=-\frac{16r^2-1}{8}<0,
\]
which is incompatible with the positive anchor value `\lambda_1(J_0)=1/8` under normalized
equality. So we may assume `b_0\neq 0`.

Write
\[
a:=16r^2-1,
\qquad
y:=(-1)^r\frac{b_{2r}}{b_0},
\qquad
q:=\frac{m^2}{n^2}>0.
\]
By Proposition 4,
\[
q=\frac{1-ay}{1+y}.
\]
Since `y\neq 0`, the quadratic factor in Proposition 4 must vanish:
\[
A_r+B_ry+C_ry^2=0.
\]
Solving for `y` in terms of `q`,
\[
y=\frac{1-q}{a+q},
\]
and substituting into the quadratic relation gives
\[
2(a+1)^3q^2
+
\bigl(3a^4-216a^3-666a^2-672a-225\bigr)q
+
a\bigl(a^4-30a^3-96a^2-98a-33\bigr)=0.
\]

Now `a\ge 143` because `r\ge 3`. Define
\[
M(a):=3a^4-216a^3-666a^2-672a-225,
\]
\[
K(a):=a^4-30a^3-96a^2-98a-33.
\]
Then
\[
M(143)=609140736>0,
\qquad
K(143)=328458240>0,
\]
and
\[
M'(a)=12a^3-648a^2-1332a-672>0,
\]
\[
K'(a)=4a^3-90a^2-192a-98>0
\]
for all `a\ge 143`. Hence `M(a)>0` and `K(a)>0` for every `a\ge 143`.

Therefore every coefficient in the polynomial
\[
2(a+1)^3q^2+M(a)q+aK(a)
\]
is strictly positive, which is impossible for `q>0`.

This contradiction shows `y=0`, hence `b_{2r}=0`.

### Meaning

This upgrades the anchored emptiness phenomenon from isolated examples to a genuine infinite
series.

Once one effective active frequency is anchored at `J_0`, no nontrivial same-parity two-term
profile
\[
b_0J_0+b_{2r}J_{2r}
\]
can share the same universal normalized shift geometry.

### Corollary 3

Let `k\ge 1`, and consider a mixed two-term profile
\[
F_{b_0,b_k}(t)=b_0J_0(t)+b_kJ_k(t),
\qquad
b_0\neq 0.
\]

If one effective active frequency carries the anchor profile `J_0` and another effective active
frequency carries `F_{b_0,b_k}`, then shared universal normalized shift series forces
\[
b_k=0.
\]

### Proof

If `k` is odd, this is Theorem 1. If `k=2r` is even, this is Theorem 4.

### Meaning

This is the first full infinite-series anchored emptiness theorem on the non-shared side.

It says that the `J_0` anchor completely eliminates every mixed two-term direction. So from the
point of view of residual bad loci, the first genuinely open anchored frontier now begins only at
support size at least `3`.

### Proposition 5

Let `k\ge 1`. A pure Bessel profile
\[
c\,J_k(t),
\qquad
c\neq 0,
\]
cannot share the same universal normalized shift series as the anchor profile `J_0`.

### Proof

If `k=2r` is even, then
\[
\lambda_1(J_k)=-\frac{4k^2-1}{8}<0,
\]
whereas
\[
\lambda_1(J_0)=\frac18>0.
\]
So normalized equality at order `1` is impossible.

If `k=2r+1` is odd, then
\[
\lambda_1(J_k)=\frac{4k^2-1}{8}.
\]
If the pure profile `J_k` shared the same universal normalized shift series as `J_0`, then
normalized equality would force
\[
\frac{4k^2-1}{p^2}=\frac{1}{n^2},
\qquad\text{so}\qquad
\frac{p^2}{n^2}=4k^2-1.
\]
But
\[
(2k-1)^2<4k^2-1<(2k)^2,
\]
so `4k^2-1` is not a square. This is impossible because `p^2/n^2` is a rational square.

### Theorem 5

Let `k\ge 1`, and consider a profile supported on `\{0,k\}`:
\[
F(t)=b_0J_0(t)+b_kJ_k(t),
\qquad
(b_0,b_k)\neq (0,0).
\]

If one effective active frequency carries the anchor profile `J_0` and another effective active
frequency carries `F`, then shared universal normalized shift series forces
\[
b_k=0.
\]

### Proof

If `b_0\neq 0`, this is Corollary 3. If `b_0=0`, this is Proposition 5.

### Meaning

This completely closes the anchored support-two sector.

So for a `J_0` anchor, every nontrivial profile supported on `\{0,k\}` is ruled out. The first
genuinely open anchored frontier therefore begins at support size at least `3`, with at least
one genuinely new mode beyond the anchor.
